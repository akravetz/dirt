# Flushing (Coco Coir)

Flushing means watering with plain, pH-adjusted water (zero nutrients, EC ~0.0) for a period before harvest. The goal is to clear residual salts from the medium and reduce the nutrient concentration in the plant's tissue, which some growers believe improves the taste and smoothness of the final product.

---

## The Debate

Flushing is one of the most contested practices in cannabis cultivation. The scientific evidence is genuinely mixed:

- **For flushing:** Growers widely report improved taste, cleaner burn, and smoother smoke after a proper flush. Anecdotal support is extensive.
- **Against flushing:** No peer-reviewed study has demonstrated that flushing meaningfully changes the chemical profile of harvested cannabis. Some researchers argue the plant's vascular system does not "purge" nutrients in the way growers imagine.
- **Middle ground:** Running correct EC throughout the grow (never overfeeding) eliminates most of what flushing is supposed to correct. If your nutrient program is well-managed, flushing is less critical. If you've been pushing high EC, a flush is a reasonable safeguard.

**Practical position for this grow:** We're running Canna A+B at relatively conservative EC (0.8–1.0 in veg; targeting 1.6–2.0 in flower) with a single-product nutrient line. Risk of salt accumulation is low. A short coco flush is reasonable and low-risk; a long flush risks late-flower nutrient starvation.

---

## Coco-Specific Flush Protocol

Coco does not bind nutrients the way soil does — it has low cation exchange capacity. This means:
- Salt buildup flushes out quickly
- A **3–7 day flush** is sufficient; do not flush for 2 weeks as commonly advised for soil
- The goal is runoff EC close to input EC (plain water input ~0.2–0.3 EC; target runoff ≤0.3)

### When to Start

Start flushing when you judge harvest is **5–7 days away** — i.e., when trichomes are at your target ratio minus one stage. Don't start the flush and then wait two weeks; the plant will cannibalize itself.

**Trichome trigger for flush:**
- If harvesting at ~20–30% amber: begin flush when you first see amber trichomes appearing (0–5%)
- Monitor daily after flush begins — once trichomes hit target ratio, harvest promptly

### Flush Procedure (Autopot)

The Autopot system changes the flush procedure — you cannot simply hand-water because the float valve will keep refilling the tray.

1. **Close the float valve** — revert to hand-watering mode for the flush period
2. Empty the reservoir (or segregate it) — do not leave nutrient solution available during flush
3. Hand-water with plain, pH-adjusted water (pH 5.8; EC 0.2–0.3 from Denver tap alone after pH correction)
4. Water volume: 500–700ml per plant, enough to get runoff into the tray
5. Remove tray runoff promptly (turkey baster/sponge) — same as the hand-watering phase
6. Repeat every 1–2 days for the 5–7 day window
7. Monitor runoff EC: target ≤0.3 by harvest day

### EC Monitoring During Flush

| Flush Day | Expected Runoff EC | Action |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| Day 1 | Likely 1.5–2.5 (releases built-up salts) | Normal; continue |
| Day 3 | Should be declining | Continue if > 0.5 |
| Day 5–7 | Target ≤ 0.3 | Harvest when trichomes are ready |

If runoff EC is stubbornly high (> 1.0) after day 3, increase water volume per session to drive more salt out.

---

## Signs of a Successful Flush

- Leaves begin to yellow and fade (natural senescence; plant consuming stored nutrients)
- This is normal and desirable — do not panic
- Avoid over-flushing: if the plant goes fully yellow-brown before you harvest, you've gone too long and terpene degradation has begun

---

## When NOT to Flush

- If EC has been well-managed throughout the grow and runoff EC is already low (< 0.8 going into late flower), a short 3-day plain water period is sufficient
- If the plant is showing any signs of stress or disease, do not add flush stress on top
- Do not flush during veg or early flower — there is no benefit and it starves the plant
