# Damping Off

Damping off is a fungal disease that kills seedlings and young plants by attacking the stem at or just below the soil/media surface. It can kill a seedling in 24–48 hours and cannot be reversed once established. Prevention is the only effective strategy.

---

## What Causes It

A group of water-mold and soil fungi — primarily **Pythium**, **Phytophthora**, **Fusarium**, and **Rhizoctonia**. These pathogens are nearly ubiquitous in growing environments; they become a problem when conditions favor their growth over the plant's:

- **High humidity (> 70% RH)** — fungal spores germinate and spread rapidly in humid air
- **Overwatering / waterlogged medium** — saturated media creates anaerobic zones where Pythium thrives
- **Cool temperatures** — below 68°F at the root zone slows plant metabolism while pathogens remain active
- **Poor airflow** — stagnant air at the canopy creates pockets of high humidity that persist
- **Contaminated equipment** — reused pots, unsterilized tools, untreated water

---

## Symptoms

### Pre-Emergence
Seeds germinate but never emerge. The sprout rots underground before breaking the surface. Appears as germination failure — no visible symptom.

### Post-Emergence (Classic Damping Off)
- Stem at the base becomes thin, soft, and discolored (gray, brown, or water-soaked)
- Seedling **suddenly topples** — the stem can no longer support the plant
- Toppled plant looks otherwise healthy (green leaves, no deficiency) — the collapse is sudden
- White fuzzy mold may appear on the medium surface around the stem base
- Adjacent seedlings may show the same progression within days

### Root Rot (Later Stage)
In slightly older plants, the same pathogens cause root rot:
- Roots turn brown, slimy, and mushy (healthy roots are white and firm)
- Plant wilts despite adequate watering — roots can't absorb water
- Yellow, drooping leaves that don't recover after watering

## Risk Assessment for This Grow

**Current risk: ELEVATED.**

RH has been running 73–76% for multiple weeks — above the 65–70% ceiling for early veg. Combined with hand-watering in coco (where overwatering risk is real), these are the two primary damping-off preconditions.

Risk factors present:
- ✅ RH > 70% — consistently
- ✅ Hand-watering phase — overwatering possible
- ✅ Young plants with developing root systems
- ✅ Standing water in trays if runoff not removed promptly

Risk mitigated by:
- ✅ Coco/perlite 60/40 — excellent drainage; does not hold water like soil
- ✅ Watering only 150–200ml per session
- ✅ Runoff removed from trays promptly
- ✅ Temperature in range (72–77°F is warm enough to keep plant metabolism ahead of pathogens)

---

## Prevention

### Humidity Control (Most Important for This Grow)
- Target RH below 65% during veg — ideally 60–65%
- Reduce humidifier output; increase exhaust fan speed
- Denver's dry ambient air (~35–40%) makes this achievable — the humidifier is working against you now
- Ensure oscillating fans create air movement at the canopy level; stagnant air is the enemy
- See [Humidity](../environment/humidity.md) for current readings and trend

### Watering Discipline
- Never water coco that is still wet — in the hand-watering phase, 150–200ml every 1–2 days is the maximum
- Remove all tray runoff within the hour; standing water in autopot trays is a direct damping-off risk
- Do not mist the stem base or splash water on it during watering

### Equipment Hygiene
- Wipe pot rims, tray surfaces, and tools with diluted hydrogen peroxide (3%) if reusing equipment
- Do not reuse rapid rooter cubes or growing media from a previous grow without sterilizing

### Airflow
- At least one oscillating fan keeping air moving through the canopy at all times
- Ensure tent intake is open enough to create gentle positive-pressure exchange

---

## Treatment

**There is no cure once a plant has damping off.** Affected seedlings will die. However, you can limit spread:

1. **Remove the affected plant immediately** — don't leave it in place while you decide what to do
2. Remove and discard any medium that the affected plant's roots touched
3. Increase airflow and drop RH in the tent immediately
4. Do not water any remaining plants for 2–3 days — let the medium dry slightly
5. If other plants were in close contact, monitor their stem bases hourly for 48 hours

For established plants showing early root rot (not full damping off), diluted **hydrogen peroxide** (3% solution, 1ml per liter of water) can be used as a root drench — it adds oxygen to the root zone and inhibits anaerobic pathogen growth. This is a symptom-management tool, not a cure.
