# Plant Training Methods

Cannabis training techniques manipulate plant structure to improve light distribution, increase bud site count, and maximize yield within a given space. Methods range from zero-stress (LST) to high-stress (topping, super cropping), and are often combined.

---

## Low Stress Training (LST)

Bending and tying branches horizontally without any cutting. The plant is redirected rather than damaged.

**How it works:** Soft ties anchor branches to pot rims or stakes, pulling them toward horizontal. The plant responds by pushing all lateral sites toward the light equally.

**Pros:**
- Zero recovery time — growth continues uninterrupted
- Safe for autoflowering plants (which can't afford veg time loss)
- 30–50% yield improvement over untrained plants
- Used in 95%+ of commercial grows in some form

**Cons:**
- Requires frequent adjustments as plants grow
- Alone (without topping) still produces one dominant cola

**Relevance to this grow:** Active on all four plants. See [LST concept page](lst.md) for timing details.

---

## Topping

Cutting the main growth tip (apical meristem) entirely, forcing the plant to develop two new dominant shoots from the next node down.

**How it works:** A clean cut above a node. The two lateral shoots below the cut become the new mains. Each subsequent top doubles the cola count (1→2→4→8).

**Pros:**
- Reliable and predictable — always produces exactly 2 new leads
- Few days recovery (3–5 days)
- Dramatically increases bud site count over multiple rounds

**Cons:**
- Not suitable for autoflowering plants — recovery time eats into a fixed veg window
- Requires healthy, established plants (4–5 nodes minimum)

**Relevance to this grow:** We're using a single top at node 4–5. Already executed or imminent as of Day 19–23. This is the foundation of our training sequence (Top → LST → SCROG).

## FIMing (F*** I Missed)

Removing approximately 75% of the new growth tip — an imprecise cut that leaves a small portion of the apical bud intact. The name comes from accidentally under-cutting a top.

**How it works:** Pinch or cut the very tip of the newest growth, leaving a small stub. The incomplete removal confuses the plant's apical dominance signal and can produce 3–4 new shoots instead of topping's reliable 2.

**Pros:**
- Can produce more new leads per operation than topping
- Slightly less stress than a full top

**Cons:**
- Less predictable — results vary; sometimes only 2 shoots develop
- Harder to execute consistently
- Offers little advantage over topping in a structured training plan

**Relevance to this grow:** Not planned. Our single-top approach is more predictable for a SCROG build.

---

## Mainlining (Manifolding)

A structured, multi-round topping technique that creates a perfectly symmetric branching structure — all colas originating from a single node junction (the "manifold"), then spread with LST.

**How it works:**
1. Top at node 3, strip everything below the manifold node
2. LST both resulting shoots horizontally
3. Top again when each shoot has 2–3 new nodes (producing 4 mains)
4. Repeat for 8 mains if desired
5. Result: 2, 4, or 8 perfectly equal colas, all at the same height

**The science:** Post-cut, sucrose floods lateral buds via the phloem. This downregulates the *BRC1* gene (branching repressor) in those buds, releasing them from apical dominance suppression. The manifold structure ensures all lateral buds receive equal sucrose signal simultaneously, creating true uniform development. ([PMC12771868](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12771868/))

**Pros:**
- Most even canopy of any training method — true cola parity
- Predictable structure for SCROG integration
- Maximizes light interception efficiency

**Cons:**
- Adds 2–3 weeks to veg time (multiple recovery periods)
- More complex to execute; requires patience and planning
- Overkill for smaller grows with fewer plants

**Relevance to this grow:** Not our current approach (single top for simplicity), but the BRC1 mechanism explains why our top is effective. Worth considering for a future run.

## Super Cropping

A controlled high-stress technique: the stem is pinched and bent until the internal woody fibers break while the outer skin remains intact. The plant heals the damage site by forming a reinforced "knuckle."

**How it works:** Pinch the stem firmly between fingers and roll/bend until you feel internal resistance give. Hold the bend until the stem stays in place. The healed knuckle becomes denser than the original tissue, improving water and nutrient transport through that junction.

**Pros:**
- Creates exceptionally strong branches capable of supporting heavy buds
- Allows targeted height reduction of branches breaking above the canopy plane
- No cutting required — lower infection risk than topping
- Can be applied mid-veg or early flower

**Cons:**
- Higher risk than LST — incorrect technique can snap the stem
- Requires some skill to judge correct pinch pressure
- Recovery takes longer than LST (no recovery time) but less than topping

**Relevance to this grow:** A strong candidate for canopy management once SCROG is installed. If any branch punches above the net plane, super cropping that branch down is cleaner than cutting. Worth adding to the toolkit.

## SCROG (Screen of Green)

A horizontal mesh screen installed above the plants. Branches are woven through screen squares as they grow, forcing a flat, even canopy across the full grow footprint.

**How it works:** As branches reach screen height, they're tucked horizontally through squares. This spreads the canopy across the net surface, filling every square with productive growth. Flip to flower when ~70% of the net is covered.

**Pros:**
- Can double or triple yields in space-limited grows
- Even canopy = even light distribution = uniform bud development
- Works synergistically with topping — the two main shoots from a top spread naturally into opposing net halves

**Cons:**
- Plants become very difficult to move once woven into the net
- Requires precise flip timing
- Setup and maintenance are more involved than untrained grows

**Relevance to this grow:** Core to our plan. VIVOSUN 4x4 net, install at ~12" plant height (weeks 6–8 veg, est. late April/early May). See [SCROG concept page](scrog.md) for timing details.

---

## SOG (Sea of Green)

The opposite philosophy from SCROG: pack many small plants tightly, skip training entirely, force each to produce one large central cola, and rely on canopy density rather than individual plant manipulation.

**How it works:** Many clones (or small seedlings) are placed in a tight grid and flipped to flower early. Each plant produces a single dominant cola. The "sea" of uniform colas fills the space.

**Pros:**
- Fast harvest cycle — minimal veg time
- Simple execution — no training required
- Works well in perpetual harvest setups

**Cons:**
- Requires many clones (or seeds) — higher input cost
- Not legal in many jurisdictions with plant count limits
- Each plant is small individually; depends entirely on volume

**Relevance to this grow:** Not applicable. Our 4-plant setup is designed around SCROG, not density. SOG would require 16–30+ plants for a 4x4 footprint.

## Recent Research

### Stress-Induced Trichome Development

Mechanical stress — including vibration, bending, and mild water restriction — may increase trichome density and potentially improve cannabinoid and terpene profiles. The proposed mechanism involves the plant upregulating secondary metabolite production (trichomes being the primary defense structure) in response to perceived physical threat.

Importantly, the PMC literature ([PMC12771868](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12771868/)) identifies this as a **major research gap**: the precise molecular pathway linking mechanical stress to trichome induction is not yet well characterized. Training techniques like LST and super cropping may therefore have secondary quality benefits beyond structural yield improvements, but this is not yet proven at the mechanistic level.

**Practical implication:** Gentle mechanical stress from LST and training is unlikely to hurt trichome development and may help — but don't over-stress plants expecting a quality boost that isn't yet confirmed.

### AI-Assisted Canopy Management

At commercial scale, sensor arrays and computer vision systems are being deployed to monitor real-time light distribution across SCROG canopies. Cameras map canopy height variation; algorithms direct robotic or human intervention (tuck here, cut there) to maintain uniform light exposure. Not relevant to our setup today but directionally interesting for where the industry is heading.
