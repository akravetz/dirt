# Isolation

**Pollen is the silent enemy of a flower harvest.** A single grain landing on a stigma in the main tent will start a calyx swelling into a seed — dropping potency, creating harsh smoke, and ruining the phenotype-evaluation signal of every plant exposed. This page is the protocol for keeping pollen contained when we run males or pollinate clones.

## The setup

| Element | Spec |
|---|---|
| **Isolation space** | Separate room with a closed door from the main tent. Confirmed available 2026-04-26. |
| **Door** | Closed at all times when males or pollinated plants are inside. Add a draft-stopper at the bottom if there's a visible gap. |
| **Ventilation** | Isolation room exhaust should not return to the main tent's intake. Easiest: window vent or basement-level exhaust. Negative pressure on the isolation room (slightly more exhaust than intake) is ideal but not required. |
| **Light** | 50–150W LED for 1–3 small males (males don't need flower-grade PAR — they just need to flower, and they flower in low light). For pollinated keeper clone: same as main tent flower light specs. |
| **Air movement** | A small clip fan during normal operation, **off** during pollen collection or pollination day. |
| **Environment** | Doesn't need to match the main tent precisely. Males tolerate 65–80 °F and 30–60% RH without complaint. Pollinated clone needs main-tent flower targets. |

## What goes in the isolation room

- Active males during their entire post-sex-confirmation life (pre-flower → pollen collection → cull).
- The flowering keeper clone during her pollination cycle (~3 weeks before through ~6 weeks after pollination).
- The dedicated portable freezer for pollen aliquots.
- Dedicated tools: scissors, paintbrush, spray bottle, parchment paper, gloves, masks, plant tags. **Tools never leave the isolation room except via decontamination.**

## What does NOT go in the isolation room

- The main tent's plants (obviously).
- Clones in the cloning station (high-humidity environment + young plants are vulnerable to airborne contaminants).
- Mother plants in veg.

## Personal hygiene (the real risk surface)

Pollen lives on hair, skin, fabric, and footwear for hours. The most common contamination path is *the human walking back and forth*. Hard rules:

### Entering the isolation room when males are pollinating or during a cross event

- **Long sleeves**, ideally a dedicated overshirt that stays in the isolation room.
- Long pants.
- Closed-toe shoes (or dedicated shoes).
- Optional: surgical mask (especially during pollen collection or paintbrush pollination).

### Leaving the isolation room *after* contact with active pollen

This is the critical window. Pollen on you = pollen in the main tent on your next visit.

1. **Spray-mist your clothes and hair** with plain water from the spray bottle (kept in the isolation room). Water denatures pollen on contact.
2. **Remove the overshirt and bag it** — leave it in the isolation room or wash separately.
3. **Shower** before going to the main tent. Wash hair specifically; pollen lodges in hair more than skin.
4. **Change into clean clothes** that haven't been in the isolation room.
5. **Wait at least 30 minutes** before entering the main tent. Lets any residual pollen on you settle and lose viability in ambient humidity.

If you've just done a paintbrush pollination, **don't enter the main tent on the same day if you can avoid it.** Delay until the next morning after a full shower.

### Tool decontamination

Tools that need to come out of the isolation room (shouldn't, but if necessary):

- **Spray-mist** with water, wipe down with isopropyl alcohol.
- **Wait 24h** before using in the main tent.
- **Better:** maintain duplicate tools — one set for isolation, one set for main tent, never crossing.

## Operational phases

The isolation room runs in distinct phases. Behavior changes per phase.

### Phase A — males growing, no pollen yet (sex confirmation → 2 weeks after flip)

- Open visits OK with normal hygiene.
- Door closed when not actively in the room.
- Tools can stay in the room or come out without decontamination (no pollen yet).

### Phase B — pollen swelling, sacs about to dehisce (1–7 days before first collection)

- **High-alert phase.** Sacs can pop unexpectedly.
- Long sleeves and mask when entering.
- Spray-mist on exit even if no active pollen handling.
- No main-tent visits within 30 min of an isolation visit.

### Phase C — active pollen collection (during the 1-week collection window)

- Full hygiene protocol (long sleeves, mask, shower on exit).
- Fan **off** during collection sessions.
- One person, focused work, no in-and-out.
- Treat the room like a chemical lab.

### Phase D — pollen banked, no active males, no pollination (between collection and cross)

- Back to Phase A rules.
- Freezer cold, sealed, no airborne pollen.
- Door closed; freezer access OK with normal hygiene.

### Phase E — pollination day + first 48h after

- Same as Phase C.
- The pollinated keeper clone is now dropping airborne pollen residue (paintbrush kicked some into the air) for the first 24h.

### Phase F — keeper clone in flower, post-pollination (pollination day + 48h → seed harvest)

- Back to Phase A rules.
- The pollinated branches are sealed events; no airborne pollen anymore.
- Routine watering and inspection are fine with normal hygiene.

## Edge cases and what to do

### A male sac opens unexpectedly outside collection window

- **Don't panic-cull.** Close the door, finish the room, then exit per Phase C protocol.
- The pollen is contained as long as the door is closed and there are no open vents to the main tent.
- Schedule a collection session for the next morning if the timing's right.

### A male shows pistils (hermie) mid-flower

- **Cull immediately.** Phase C exit protocol.
- Discard ALL pollen previously collected from that male (see [male-evaluation.md](male-evaluation.md)).
- Decontaminate the isolation room (HEPA + wipe-down) before introducing the keeper clone.

### Suspected pollen contamination of the main tent

Symptoms: random calyx swelling on multiple plants outside the keeper, white pistils retracting/browning early.

- **Do not panic.** Identify which plants are affected.
- Affected plants will produce some seeded bud — still smokable, lower potency, harsher.
- Cause analysis: trace your last few isolation-room visits and identify the protocol break.
- For breeding outcome: any unintended seeds are *not* trustable for breeding — pollen source unknown, parentage unknown. Cull these seeds; don't add to the seed bank.

### Power outage in the isolation room (>4 hours)

- The portable freezer rises above safe pollen-storage temp.
- Once power restored: check freezer temp probe. If above -10 °C for >2 hours, viability is compromised.
- Decision: discard the pollen and re-collect from a fresh male, or accept reduced viability and verify on a sacrificial stigma before using in a real cross.
