# Pollen Handling

The full lifecycle of pollen from a male's first dehiscing sac to a paintbrush stroke on a female stigma — sometimes months or years later.

## Lifecycle overview

```
collection → drying → flour-cut → aliquoting → freezer storage → thaw (single use) → pollination
   day 0      day 0–2    day 2       day 2          day 2 → use      ~30 min before use
```

## Pollen viability vs storage method (reference)

| Storage | Conditions | Viability |
|---|---|---|
| Open air | Room temp, ambient humidity | 1–2 weeks |
| Sealed at room temp | Airtight container, dry | 2–4 weeks |
| Refrigerator | 4 °C, sealed, with desiccant | 3–6 months |
| **Freezer (our target)** | **-18 °C, flour-cut 4:1, vacuum-sealed** | **18–24 months reliably** |
| Liquid N₂ | -196 °C, cryo protocol | 8+ years documented |

Our target window: 18–24 months minimum. We won't need cryo for a hobby program.

## Collection

### Parent identity

Pollen is only useful if its parentage stays unambiguous. Treat each confirmed keeper male as a separate pollen lot from the moment sacs begin swelling.

Assign male IDs at sex confirmation, before collection starts: `TA-M1`, `TA-M2`, `TA-M3`, etc. Use the same ID everywhere: plant tag, male-evaluation table, parchment label, drying container, aliquot tube, freezer box, and cross record.

Minimum notes before collection: male ID, source population (`SBxBS01 Track A`), sex-confirmation date, flip date, first visible sac-swelling date, collection session/date, and quick [male-evaluation](male-evaluation.md) score summary. If two males both look worth preserving, collect and freeze them separately. Do not blend male pollen unless the breeding goal is explicitly a mixed-sire seed lot.

### Timing

- Wait until ~33% of visible sacs are dehiscing (cracking open and shedding visible yellow pollen). Earlier = pollen not ready, lower viability. Later = sacs already shed, less collection yield.
- Collect over **3 sessions** spaced 2–3 days apart (e.g., days 1, 4, 7) — sacs dehisce non-concurrently, so multiple passes capture more.
- **Time of day:** mid-morning, after RH drops below ~50%. High humidity = clumpy pollen. Avoid right after lights-on (humidity is highest then in the isolation room).
- For the current Track A speed run, expect first useful pollen roughly **2–4 weeks after the 12/12 flip**, with the exact date driven by male maturity and sac development.

### Method

You'll need:

- Parchment paper or wax paper, ~1 ft sq sheets (one per male per session)
- Tweezers, sterile
- Small soft brush (artist's #2 round) — optional, for sweeping sacs
- Latex gloves
- Surgical mask (so you don't sneeze pollen onto the sheet)

Procedure:

1. **In the isolation room, with the door closed and fan off** — eliminate airflow that would scatter pollen.
2. Lay parchment paper on a flat surface under the male's flowering colas.
3. Gently shake the colas, or use the brush to sweep mature open sacs onto the paper.
4. Use tweezers to remove individual swollen-but-unopened sacs and place them on the parchment to dehisce naturally over the next 24h.
5. Close the parchment loosely and label it with male ID + collection date.

Keep one parchment sheet and one drying container per male per session. If pollen from `TA-M1` touches tools or paper used for `TA-M2`, the lot becomes contaminated and cannot be used for clean parentage tracking.

### Yield expectation

A small, focused male can produce **0.5–2 g of pollen** total over a 1-week collection window. That's enough to pollinate dozens of branches. We need maybe 0.1–0.2 g for a single cross — pollen is wildly abundant once a male is at peak.

## Drying

Pollen straight from a sac is too moist to freeze without ice-crystal damage to the gametes.

1. After collection, transfer the parchment to a sealed container with a **dry rice or silica desiccant** packet.
2. Hold at room temp (~70 °F) for **24–48 hours** with the desiccant.
3. Pollen should look like fine powder, not clumpy. Tap the parchment — clumps mean it's still too moist; let it dry another 12–24h.

Don't skip this step. Wet pollen freezes badly and loses 50–80% viability vs properly dried pollen.

## Flour cut (4:1)

Mix dried pollen with **baked all-purpose flour** at a 4:1 ratio (4 parts flour, 1 part pollen, by volume).

### Why flour?

- **Bulking agent** — pollen is microscopic and small amounts are hard to handle. Flour adds mass.
- **Desiccant** — flour absorbs ambient moisture and protects the pollen during freeze-thaw cycles.
- **Carrier** — when applied with a paintbrush, the flour distributes the pollen evenly across the stigma rather than dumping it all at one spot.

### How to bake the flour

Pollen-grade flour must be **sterilized of moisture and any microbial contamination**:

1. Spread flour ~1cm thick on a baking sheet.
2. Bake at **250 °F / 120 °C for 30 minutes**.
3. Cool to room temperature in a dry environment.
4. Store in airtight container until use.

A single batch (1 cup of flour) covers many breeding events.

### Mixing

In a small clean dish (e.g. a glass shot glass):
1. Add 4 parts flour by volume.
2. Add 1 part dried pollen.
3. Gently swirl/fold with a clean toothpick. Don't shake hard — pollen grains are fragile.

## Aliquoting

**Don't freeze the whole stash as one batch.** Every freeze-thaw cycle damages pollen viability. The fix: divide into many small single-use aliquots, freeze them all, thaw only the one you need.

### Aliquot containers

- **Microcentrifuge tubes (1.5 mL Eppendorf tubes)** — ideal. Cheap, watertight, fit a freezer drawer easily.
- Alternative: small glass vials with screw-top lids (5 mL).
- Avoid: ziploc bags (poor seal, frost contamination over months).

### Aliquot size

For a single cross event (1–2 branches pollinated): **1/4–1/2 teaspoon of flour-cut pollen mix** is more than enough. Each aliquot tube should hold roughly that — call it 0.5–2 mL of the 4:1 mix. Err smaller on the first batch so a single thaw does not waste the whole collection.

A typical collection batch yields **8–15 aliquots**. That's 8–15 separate cross events possible from one male.

### Aliquot strategy for the first Track A batch

Default to more small tubes, not fewer large tubes: 2 tiny test aliquots per male for viability/practice, 8–12 standard branch aliquots for one lower-branch pollination each, and 1–2 reserve aliquots that stay untouched unless the line depends on them.

If total pollen is low, prioritize **separate identity over volume**. A small clean `TA-M1` lot and a small clean `TA-M2` lot are more useful than one larger blended lot.

### Labeling

Every aliquot gets:
- Male ID (e.g. `TA-M2`)
- Strain/population (`SBxBS01 Track A`)
- Collection date or collection range
- Aliquot number (`01/12`)
- Mix ratio (`4:1 flour:pollen`)

Example: `TA-M2 SBxBS01 2026-06-18 03/12 4:1`.

Permanent marker on the side, plus a dated label on the freezer box. Put the same ID in the inventory table before the tube goes into the freezer.

## Storage

### Hardware

- **Dedicated portable freezer** for breeding-only use. Digital thermostat. Target -18 °C / 0 °F.
- Located in the male isolation room, NOT the kitchen freezer (door-cycling = thermal swings = viability loss).
- Inventory log on the freezer's lid (which aliquots are inside, what's been used).

### Inventory log format

A simple sheet inside the freezer, or a wiki page (`breeding/projects/<project>/pollen-inventory.md`):

| Aliquot | Male | Strain | Collected | Used | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| TA-M2-01/12 | TA-M2 | SBxBS01 Track A | 2026-06-22 | — | full |
| TA-M2-02/12 | TA-M2 | SBxBS01 Track A | 2026-06-22 | 2026-12-15 | used on keeper K1 lower-left branch |
| ... | | | | | |

## Thawing — single-use discipline

When you're ready to pollinate:

1. Pull *one* aliquot from the freezer. Leave the rest alone.
2. Let it warm to room temperature in its sealed tube — **at least 30 min**, ideally 1 hour.
3. **Do not open the tube while it's still cold** — atmospheric moisture will condense on the cold pollen and ruin it.
4. Once at room temp, open and use immediately for pollination (see [cross-procedure.md](cross-procedure.md)).
5. Any unused pollen from that aliquot: discard. **Do not refreeze.** Refrozen pollen is dead pollen.

**Treat each aliquot as a one-shot consumable.** This is why we make 8–15 of them.

## Verification (optional, recommended)

If you want to confirm viability before banking long-term, do a wet test on a sacrificial female stigma:

- Take a clone or low-priority female plant, paint a single stigma with fresh pollen, mark it.
- 5–10 days later, the stigma should be brown/withered (pollination response). A green/white stigma after 10 days = pollen wasn't viable.

This isn't strictly necessary if you follow the protocol above, but for a first batch it's a sanity check.

## Discard criteria

Discard a pollen aliquot if:

- It was thawed and not used (don't refreeze).
- The freezer lost power for more than 4 hours.
- Frost is visible inside the tube (water vapor got in — pollen is dead).
- The male turned out hermaphroditic late in flower — discard *all* pollen from that male, see [male-evaluation.md](male-evaluation.md).
- More than 24 months in the freezer (replace from a fresh collection if possible).
