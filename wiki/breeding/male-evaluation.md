# Male Plant Evaluation

Males don't grow buds, so the temptation is to treat them as interchangeable pollen factories. **They're not.** A male contributes 50% of every offspring's genome — the same axes that matter in females (color, terps, vigor, structure) are encoded just as much in males, even though males don't visibly express them in flower form.

This page is the evaluation protocol for picking *which* male's pollen we collect, before culling the others.

## Sex identification

Males show pre-flowers ~3–4 weeks from germination — typically 1–2 weeks earlier than females. This is one of the few advantages of regular seeds: you find out fast.

**Where to look:** node 4–6, where the petiole meets the main stem. Use a 60× loupe in good light.

| Male pre-flower | Female pre-flower |
|---|---|
| Egg/spade-shaped | Tear-drop with single white pistil emerging |
| Hangs on a tiny stalk | Sits flush against the node |
| Smooth surface | Has a visible pistil |

When in doubt: wait 3–4 more days. A maturing male sac becomes obviously round and ball-like; a female stays tear-drop and starts showing pistils.

## Critical timing window

Once a male is confirmed, you have **~7–10 days** before the first sacs open and start releasing pollen. Sacs releasing pollen in the same room as a flowering female = unwanted pollination = ruined flower.

**At sex confirmation: move the male to the isolation room, immediately. Do not wait.** See [isolation.md](isolation.md) for the room setup.

## Male evaluation criteria

Score each surviving male over the 2–4 weeks between sex confirmation and pollen-sac swelling. The male whose pollen you actually collect is the one that scores best on the criteria that matter for the cross.

### 1. Stem and petiole color

The single best phenotype proxy for anthocyanin contribution.

- Purple stem = carries anthocyanin alleles, will pass them to offspring.
- Green stem = doesn't necessarily mean *no* anthocyanin (it could be heterozygous and not expressing), but a purple-stemmed male is a much surer bet.
- For our SBxBS01 program where color is a primary axis: **purple stems are required for keeper-male status**.

### 2. Vigor

How fast did he grow from seedling to sex confirmation? Fast = vigor, which is heritable. Compare against the females you culled and the other males.

- Days from germ to first true leaves
- Week-over-week node count
- Stem thickness at sex confirmation

### 3. Structure

Same axes as female evaluation, but evaluated as proxies for what offspring might inherit:

- Internode distance — for the 2026-05-02 stabilization target, longer/open internodes are preferred because offspring should train cleanly through a trellis. Avoid both squat indica structure and uncontrolled stretch.
- Branching pattern — symmetrical, lateral branching is generally desirable.
- Apical dominance vs even canopy.

### 4. Leaf morphology

Indica-leaning leaves (broad, dark) vs sativa-leaning (narrow, light) — useful as a hereditary signal. For the current line, sativa-leaning morphology is preferred if the male also passes the purple-stem and hermie gates.

### 5. Aroma — stem rub

Yes, males have terpenes. Most amateur breeders skip this; it's a real signal.

- At sex confirmation and again at pollen-sac swelling, pinch a leaf petiole or stem and smell.
- Note dominant terps — gas, fruit, floral, earth, pine.
- A male whose stem rub matches what you want in the offspring's smell is a real candidate. A male who smells "off" (rotten, chemical, weak) is not.

### 6. Pollen-sac density and structure

A male producing dense clusters of large sacs > a male with sparse, small sacs. Heritable, and predicts offspring vigor in seed production. Less critical than the above.

### 7. Hermaphrodite tendency (deal-breaker)

A male showing any pistils at all = self-pollinating tendency in the line = cull. Hermaphrodite genetics are the single most insidious flaw to breed forward; they make every future seed in the line at risk of selfing under stress. **Zero tolerance.**

Score 1–10 per axis. **Stem color is gating** for our color-priority program — a green-stemmed male is automatically deprioritized regardless of other scores. **Sativa-leaning, trellis-friendly structure is a strong preference** for the current stabilization line. **Hermie tendency is also gating** — any pistils visible = immediate cull.

Pick the highest scorer that passes both gates. The others get culled before any sacs open.

## When to cull losing males

The instant pollen-sac swelling becomes visible on the *winner* — the moment you commit to collecting his pollen — cull the others by removing them from the isolation room and disposing of them outside. Don't compost or leave near the grow.

Reason: a losing male in the same room as a winner introduces contamination risk during pollen collection, and adds environmental load (heat, transpiration) without benefit.

## Edge cases

- **All males score badly** — pop a second batch of regulars. Don't compromise on a poor male; his genetics are 50% of the offspring.
- **Only one male survives the regulars pack** — score him alone against thresholds. If he passes the gates (purple stem, no hermie), use him. If he fails, pop more.
- **Two males score equally well** — collect pollen from both, label aliquots separately. They become parallel lines you can compare in F2.
- **A male shows pistils late (after pollen collection started)** — discard ALL the pollen collected from that plant. Hermie genetics in pollen = hermie offspring. Hard rule.
