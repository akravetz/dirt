# Cross Procedure

The actual cross — applying banked pollen to a flowering female and harvesting the resulting seeds. Assumes you have:

- A rooted clone of the keeper female, flowering in the isolation room (NOT the main tent).
- A frozen pollen aliquot from your selected male, see [pollen-handling.md](pollen-handling.md).
- Branch-level labeling intent (we pollinate 1–2 branches, not the whole plant — see *Why selective* below).

## Why selective branch pollination, not whole-plant

Three reasons we pollinate only one or two branches, never the whole keeper:

1. **Most of the plant produces normal smokable flower.** Seedy bud is harsh and lower-potency; we want the keeper's *bud* harvest as well as her *seed* harvest.
2. **Seed yield needed is small.** A single branch produces 100–500 seeds depending on size. We need ~50 to pheno-hunt; we don't need 5000.
3. **Decoupling.** If something goes wrong with the cross (pollen wasn't viable, stigma timing was off), only a branch is wasted. The plant still produces flower.

## When to pollinate (timing)

- **Female stigma stage:** stigmas (the white/cream "hairs" sticking out of calyxes) must be **freshly emerged, white, and sticky**. This is roughly **week 2–3 of flower** for most strains (10–21 days after the 12/12 flip).
- **Too early** (week 0–1): not enough flower formed; few stigmas to receive pollen.
- **Too late** (week 4+): stigmas browning and retracting; receptivity drops fast. After week 5, success rate is low.

If the keeper's clone was flipped on the same day as expected, plan to pollinate around **day 14–18 of flower**.

## Setup before pollination day

The day before:

1. Confirm pollen aliquot is identified, in freezer, ready.
2. Pre-position the keeper clone in the isolation room (already there from flower start).
3. Identify which branches you'll pollinate. Pick **lower-tier branches**:
   - Lower colas are smaller, so fewer total seeds (which is what you want for first cross).
   - Lower branches are easier to isolate physically with a bag/cone if you want to limit drift.
   - Selecting 1 lower-left + 1 lower-right gives you two independent pollination sites for redundancy.
4. **Pre-print labels** for each branch: parent pair (`M2 × K1`), date, branch position. Plant tags or jeweler's tags work.

## Pollination day (procedure)

### Equipment

- Thawed pollen aliquot (pulled from freezer ~1 hour earlier, see [pollen-handling.md](pollen-handling.md))
- Small soft paintbrush — artist's #2 round, dedicated to this purpose, never washed with anything that contained moisture
- Plant tags + permanent marker
- Latex gloves
- Surgical mask (pollen is reactive on skin and respiratory tissue; also reduces stray pollen from your breath)
- Spray bottle with plain water (for after — denatures stray pollen on contact)
- A piece of parchment paper or small bag to wrap the pollinated branch (if you want to extra-isolate)

### Steps

1. **Suit up.** Gloves + mask. Long sleeves. Don't go anywhere else after this until you've showered and changed.
2. **Open the pollen aliquot.** It's at room temp — should be fine flour-pollen mix consistency, not clumpy.
3. **Dip paintbrush into the pollen mix.** Tap off excess; you want the bristles dusted, not loaded.
4. **Paint each pistil.** Gentle dabbing motion across the white sticky stigmas of the chosen branch. Cover thoroughly but don't drown them. Spend 30–60 seconds per branch.
5. **Tag the branch immediately** with the printed label. Cross + date + branch position.
6. **(Optional) Bag the branch** loosely with a small paper or organza bag for 24 hours. This prevents stray pollen drift from this branch to other branches on the same plant — useful if you only want certain branches seeded.
7. **Repeat for branch 2** (different parent pairing optional, e.g. M2 left and M3 right if you collected pollen from two males).
8. **Close the pollen tube. Discard any unused pollen from this aliquot — don't refreeze.**
9. **Spray-mist the rest of the plant** with plain water. Water denatures any stray pollen grains on contact, protecting the unpollinated branches. Mist gently.
10. **Spray-mist your gloves and mask** before removing. Bag them; don't reuse.
11. **Shower and change clothes** before re-entering the main tent or cloning station. Pollen lives on hair, skin, and fabric for hours.

### Cross-contamination scenarios to prevent

- Pollen from one branch drifting to another branch on the same plant — prevented by bagging or by spray-misting after.
- Pollen on your clothes/skin riding into the main tent on the next visit — prevented by post-pollination shower.
- Two-male cross-contamination (M2 pollen lands on M3-targeted branch) — prevented by doing branches one at a time, with a fresh paintbrush for each male, and spray-misting between.

## Verifying the pollination took

- **24–48 hours after:** stigmas on pollinated branches should start browning and retracting. This is the pollination response.
- **5–7 days after:** browned stigmas; calyxes start swelling visibly compared to unpollinated branches.
- **2–3 weeks after:** seed shells are visibly forming inside the calyxes. By this point you can confirm seed development by gently squeezing a calyx — you should feel hard seed shells.

If 7 days post-pollination the stigmas are still white and the calyxes haven't changed: **pollen was not viable** or the timing window was missed. Options: pollinate a different branch with a fresh aliquot, or wait for the next cycle.

## Seed maturation

Seeds need **4–6 weeks** to mature after successful pollination. They go through three stages visible from the outside:

1. **Soft white** (week 1–2): pollination has taken; calyxes swelling but seeds still soft.
2. **Tan striped** (week 3–4): seeds firming up, dark stripes appearing. Not yet viable.
3. **Dark with tiger striping** (week 5–6): mature, viable. Hard to the touch. Tan-with-dark-stripes is the harvest signal.

The keeper's overall flower harvest is typically week 9–10 of flower. If pollinated at day 14, seeds mature around week 8 — a week or two before regular harvest. **Harvest the pollinated branches separately when seeds are mature**, even if the rest of the plant isn't done.

## Seed harvest

1. **Cut the pollinated branch off** at maturity (week 8–9 typically).
2. **Hang to dry** in a paper bag for 7–14 days, similar to flower drying. The bag catches any seeds that fall out as the calyxes dry and split.
3. **Once dry, manually break apart the calyxes** over a clean sheet. Seeds will fall out. Sort.
4. **Cull the duds**: pale, soft, undersized, or cracked seeds get discarded. Mature viable seeds are tan with dark stripes, hard, ~3–5 mm long.

### Yield expectation

- A single pollinated mid-sized branch: **100–300 seeds**.
- A single pollinated lower-tier branch: **50–150 seeds**.

For a 2-branch cross, expect **150–500 seeds total** — enough for many future grows.

## Storage

- **Cool, dark, dry.** Standard cannabis seed storage.
- Sealed glass jar with silica desiccant, in the dedicated breeding freezer (not the same compartment as pollen — different humidity needs).
- **Long-term:** -18 °C in airtight container. Cannabis seeds are viable for 5+ years properly stored, longer in a freezer.

### Labeling

Every seed jar:

- Cross: `M2 × K1` (where M2 and K1 are the male and female IDs)
- Strain ancestry: `(SBxBS01) F2`
- Harvest date
- Source branch (e.g. `M2 × K1, lower-left`)
- Seed count

Maintain an inventory page in the project folder.

## What you've made

The seeds from this cross are **F2** in our nomenclature (see [nomenclature.md](nomenclature.md)). They will express maximum genetic variance — every seed is a unique recombination of SB and BS01 alleles. Some will lean SB; some will lean BS01; most will be in between but with novel trait combinations.

This is the raw material for the next pheno hunt cycle, where you select F2 keepers and decide whether to F3-stabilize, BX, or just enjoy what you got.
